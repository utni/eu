Hierarchy of Sources in EU Law

Law and Instutions of the EU week 5y

Introduction: A Sui Generis Legal Order

  • ➤A unique (sui generis) and autonomous system, distinct from public international law and national legal systems.
  • ➤The EU Treaties do not explicitly codify a formal hierarchy of norms.
  • ➤The hierarchy is largely developed by the Court of Justice of the European Union (CJEU) to ensure the effectiveness (effet utile) of EU law.
  • ➤The supremacy of EU law over national law is the most fundamental outcome of this hierarchy.

Part 1: The Apex of the Hierarchy: Primary Law

  • ➤The Founding Treaties (TEU, TFEU) & Protocols: Considered the EU's "constitutional charter," they establish the objectives, values, institutions, and competences of the Union.
  • ➤The Charter of Fundamental Rights: Elevated to the same legal value as the Treaties by the Treaty of Lisbon.
  • ➤General Principles of Law: Unwritten sources developed by the CJEU with primary law status (e.g., legal certainty, proportionality).

The Charter

  • ➤Pre-Lisbon: A solemn political declaration without binding legal force.
  • ➤Post-Lisbon (2009): Granted the same legal value as the Treaties (Art. 6 TEU).
  • ➤Function: Acts as a key benchmark for reviewing the legality of actions by EU institutions and Member States when implementing EU law.
  • ➤Origin: A response to concerns from national courts, notably Germany's in its Solange jurisprudence.

Part 2: The Intermediate Layer: International Agreements

  • ➤Place in the Hierarchy:
    ▼ Subordinate to Primary Law
    ▲ Superior to Secondary Law
  • ➤Binding Effect: Agreements concluded by the EU are binding on its institutions and on the Member States.
  • ➤Consequence: All secondary EU legislation must comply with the international agreements to which the Union is a party.

The Landmark Case: Kadi

  • ➤Context: Challenges to EU regulations that implemented UN Security Council sanctions.
  • ➤The CJEU's Ruling: EU legal acts, even when implementing international obligations, remain subject to review based on the EU's own constitutional principles, particularly fundamental rights.
  • ➤Key Takeaway: The ruling affirmed the autonomy of the EU legal order, with primary law functioning as a "constitutional shield."

Part 3: The Workhorse of EU Governance: Secondary Law

  • ➤Regulations: Directly applicable, binding in their entirety. (Goal: Uniformity)
  • ➤Directives: Binding as to the result, but form and methods left to Member States. (Goal: Harmonization)
  • ➤Decisions: Binding only on those to whom they are addressed. (Goal: Specific application)
  • ➤Non-Binding Acts ("Soft Law"): Recommendations and Opinions.

Article 288 TFEU: The Legal Acts of the Union

"To exercise the Union's competences, the institutions shall adopt regulations, directives, decisions, recommendations and opinions."
  • Regulation: general application, binding in its entirety, directly applicable.
  • Directive: binding as to the result, but leaves choice of form and methods to Member States.
  • Decision: binding in its entirety on those to whom it is addressed.
  • Recommendations & Opinions: no binding force.

Hierarchy Within Secondary Law (Post-Lisbon)

  1. Legislative Acts: Adopted via legislative procedure. Top of the secondary hierarchy.
  2. Delegated Acts (Art. 290 TFEU): Supplement or amend "non-essential elements" of a legislative act.
  3. Implementing Acts (Art. 291 TFEU): Ensure uniform implementation of Union acts.

Academic Debate (ref. J. Bast): The relationship between delegated and implementing acts is arguably a horizontal divide, not a vertical one, distinguished by function and control, not legal rank.

Article 290 TFEU: Delegated Acts

  • ➤A legislative act may delegate power to the Commission to adopt **non-legislative acts** of general application.
  • ➤**Function:** These acts can **supplement or amend** certain **non-essential elements** of the legislative act.
  • ➤**Strict Conditions:** The legislative act must explicitly define the objectives, content, scope, and duration of the delegation.
  • ➤**Control:** The Parliament or Council can object to or revoke the delegation ('power of scrutiny').

Article 291 TFEU: Implementing Acts

  • ➤Used where **uniform conditions** for implementing legally binding Union acts are needed.
  • ➤**Function:** These acts do **not** amend or supplement the basic act; they merely provide for its uniform implementation across the EU.
  • ➤**Power Conferred:** Powers are conferred on the Commission (or, in specific cases, the Council).
  • ➤**Control:** Member States control the Commission's exercise of these powers through committees ("Comitology").

Part 4: The Role of the Judiciary & Interpretive Doctrines

  • ➤CJEU Case Law: A de facto source of law that is essential for operationalizing the hierarchy.
  • ➤Direct Effect (Van Gend en Loos): Ability of individuals to rely on EU law provisions before national courts.
  • ➤Indirect Effect / Consistent Interpretation (Von Colson): Duty of national courts to interpret national law, "as far as possible," in line with the relevant EU directive.

The Limits of Directives and Judicial Solutions

  • ➤The Problem (Marshall Ruling): Directives cannot, in principle, have "horizontal direct effect" in disputes between private individuals.
  • ➤The Solutions: The CJEU bridges this gap by:
    • A broad interpretation of "the State" (emanation of the state doctrine).
    • Systematic application of indirect effect.
    • Acknowledging "incidental" horizontal effects.

Part 5: The Keystone: The Principle of Primacy

  • ➤Established Judicially in Costa v ENEL (1964): The principle is not explicitly written in the Treaties.
  • ➤Meaning: In a conflict, EU law prevails over all national law, including national constitutions.
  • ➤Simmenthal Ruling: A national court has a duty to immediately set aside any conflicting provision of national law.
  • ➤The CJEU's Perspective: Primacy is an absolute, unconditional principle.

The Dialogue with National Courts

  • ➤Conditional Acceptance: National constitutional courts accept primacy, but with conditions based on their own constitutional orders.
  • ➤German Perspective (Solange): Primacy is accepted "as long as" the EU provides equivalent fundamental rights protection.
  • ➤Italian Perspective (Controlimiti): Primacy cannot breach "fundamental principles" or "inalienable rights" of the Italian constitution.

Conclusion

  • ➤The EU law hierarchy is a dynamic system shaped by CJEU case law, not a rigid pyramid.
  • ➤It is a multi-layered structure: Primary Law > International Agreements > Secondary Law.
  • ➤The principle of Primacy is the subject of an ongoing "constitutional dialogue" between the CJEU and Member State constitutional courts.
  • ➤This dialogue balances EU autonomy with the constitutional identities of its Member States.
